\section{Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs)} % (fold)
\label{sec:chapKeyWord__section_2}

Compounds including transition metals from group VI and chalcogens are called transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) and are usually represented by $MX_2$, with M a transition-metal atom (Mo, W, etc) and X a chalcogen atom (S, Se, or Te). Layered compounds are promising candidates for atomically thin structures. Like graphite, transition metal dichalcogenides are composed of a stack of 2D layers and those are weakly coupled by van der Waals interactions. As a result, it is relatively easy to exfoliate atomically thin layer (or monolayer) from bulk TMDs.

\subsection{Crystal structure of TMDs} % (fold)
\label{sub:chapKeyWord__subsection_2_1}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/TMD_2H.png}
	\caption{The 2H phase of TMDs (a)Top view, (b) side view}
	\label{fig:TMD_2H}
\end{figure}

In the bulk form, TMDs have various crystal structures. There are many kinds of crystal sturctures depending on the symmetries and repeating unit and coordination such as 1T, 2H and 3R. The most common crystal structures in TMDs are the 2H phase. The 2H phase is typically the most stable and thermodynamically favorable phase for TMDs. This is because the 2H phase has a lower energy compared to the other phases, which can be atrributed to its hexagonal close-packed structure with strong covalent bonding within the layers and weaker van der Waals forces between the layers. In Figure \ref{fig:TMD_2H}, various views of the 2H phase are shown from different angles. In top view, the 2H phase TMD has a honeycomb lattice structure that is similar to graphene. However, unlike graphene, which consists of a single layer of carbon atoms, the 2H phase TMD has multiple layers of transition metal atoms and chalcogen atoms stacked on top of each other. Since our focus was limited to monolayer TMDs in this study, we only discuss the structure of monolayer TMDs.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/TMD_2D.png}
	\caption{Representation of the structure of transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) (a) three-dimensional representation, (b) two-dimensional representation (side view)}
	\label{fig:TMD_2D}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:TMD_2D}, Single layer of TMDs is composed of three hexagonally packed atomic layers where a layer of metal atoms (M) is sandwiched between two layers of chalcogen atoms (X). The atoms of metals and chalcogens within a single layer form covalent bonds, and in the bulk each layer is weakly attached to adjacent layers by van der Waals forces. Due to the stronger in-plane interactions between atoms compared to those along the out-of-plane direction, it is relatively simple to isolate a monolayer from a bulk material through exfoliation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/TMD_BZ.png}
	\caption{First Brillouin zone (BZ) of Transition-metal dichalcogenides.}
	\label{fig:TMD_BZ}
\end{figure}

The in-plane lattice vectors of TMDs are oriented at an angle of 120 degrees with respect to each other. The reciprocal lattice vectors that can be derived from the lattice vectors of TMDs are depicted in Figure \ref{fig:TMD_BZ}. The two reciprocal lattice vectors are oriented at an angle of 120 degrees with respect to each other and they make hexagonal Brillouin zone.  In the Brillouin zone, there are several symmetry points which are electronically important in $MX_2$ compounds including $M$,$K$ and $\Gamma$ points.

\subsection{Electronic structure of TMDs} % (fold)
\label{sub:chapKeyWord__subsection_2_2}

Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) are a class of two-dimensional materials that have been extensively studied due to their unique electronic and optical properties. The band structure of TMDs varies depending on the number of layers in the material. Figure \ref{fig:TMD_band} represents electronic structure of $MoS_2$ which is one of the TMDs materials.

While bulk materials of transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) in the hexagonal phase exhibit an indirect bandgap, altering the material's thickness brings about a modification in its electronic structure. Specifically, the position of the minimum energy point in the conduction band and the maximum energy point in the valence band can be changed. In the year 2010, research demonstrated that a single-layer of molybdenum disulfide ($MoS_2$) possesses a direct bandgap at specific points (labeled as $K$ points) within the hexagonal Brillouin zone. However, when the material is in a bilayer or thicker form, it maintains an indirect bandgap behavior. This alteration in the band structure is attributed to quantum confinement effects. These effects are more pronounced for the wavefunctions engaged in indirect transitions, which is the reason behind the observed shift in the band structure. Figure \ref{fig:TMD_band} illustrates the outcomes of Density Functional Theory calculations for the electronic band structure of $MoS_2$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/TMD_band.png}
    \caption{Calculated band structure of MoS2 for different thicknesses. A direct bandgap occurs in the monolayer at the K points, whereas the bandgap is indirect for two or more layers. The solid arrows indicate the lowest energy transition between the valence band and the conduction band in each case, defining the bandgap \cite{fabian2022}.}
    \label{fig:TMD_band}
\end{figure}

Illustration of the arrangement of electronic band alignments near the essential band gap of a monolayer of WS2 is shown in Figure \ref{fig:brightdark}. As with other TMD materials, monolayer TMDs $WS_2$ has a direct band-gap at $K^+$ and$K^-$ points of the Brillouin zone (BZ). It has strong spin orbit coupling because of heavy metal atoms. It leads to band splitting in valence band (VB) and conduction band (CB). The electronic band become splitted and spin-polarized thanks to the broken inversion symmetry in mono-layer limit. The amplitude of band splitting in valence band (VB) is around 400 meV. In case of conduction band, its magnitude is about 30 meV in monolayer $WS_2$ \cite{kormanyos2015k}.

In monolayer $WS_2$, the transition between uppermost valence band subband and lowest conduction band subband is optically inactive. As a result, the lowest energy transition of A-exciton is opically dark and the upper subband of the conduction band (CB) is associated with the lowest energy bright A-exciton. The TMD materials which have these properties are called darkish TMD and monolayer $WSe_2$ and $MoS_2$ belong to darkish monolayers. In contrast, other TMD materials such as $MoSe_2$ and $MoTe_2$ have opposite arrangement in sub-band of CB and the lowest energy component of A-exciton is optically bright. 

The most difference between bright and darkish monoalyers is low-temperature photoluminescence spectra. Bright monolayers exhibit a straightforward photoluminescence (PL) spectrum attributed to neutral and charged excitons. In contrast, darkish monolayers present a variety of additional emission peaks originating from localized or bound excitons.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/brightdark.png}
    \caption{Diagram of relevant sub-bands in the conduction and valence bands at the $K^+$, $K^-$, and $\Lambda$ points of the Brillouin zone in monolayer sub-bands. The red and blue arrows shows the $X_A$ and $X_B$ \cite{molas2017optical}.}
    \label{fig:brightdark}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:darkpl} illustrates the optical response of monolayer $WS_2$ at low temperature ($\sim 5K$). Normally, there are two optical resonances from neutral ($X_A$) and charged ($T_1$) excitons. However, in the photoluminescence(PL) spectra, there are many peaks below these two main peaks that dominate the absorption. This is because the exciton ground state of monolayer $WS_2$ is optically dark. In this kind of monolayer TMDs, there are many kinds of extra recombination channels such as localized excitons, donor-acceptor recombination and phonon-assisted recombination. T2 is believed to represent impurity-bound excitons, while the peaks L1, L2, L3, and L4 are tentatively associated with localized or bound excitons.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figure/Chapter_Lipsum/darkpl.png}
    \caption{Measured spectra of $WS_2$ monolayer: Photoluminescence (PL, blue dashed curve), photoluminescence excitation (PLE, red dashed curve) and Reflectance (RC, black solid curve) at low temperature ($\sim 5K$) \cite{molas2017optical}.}
    \label{fig:darkpl}
\end{figure}
